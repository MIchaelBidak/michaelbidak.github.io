8 Signs It’s Time to Call a Headhunter (and What to Do Next)

Most companies who bring in a headhunter say the same thing on our first call: “We probably should have done this two months ago.” That’s not a criticism — it’s just how it tends to go. A search rarely fails loudly. It stalls quietly, one “let’s circle back next week” at a time, while everyone involved gets back to doing their actual jobs.

So here’s the list I wish more teams kept taped to the wall. One of these on its own is just a Tuesday. Two or three at once is a pattern — and patterns are worth a fifteen-minute phone call.

1. The role has been open for more than 60 days.

Somewhere around the two-month mark, an open req stops being a search and starts being furniture. The work hasn’t gone anywhere, though — it’s been quietly landing on the rest of the team, and they’ve noticed. If you can’t name what changed in your approach between month one and month two, nothing did.

2. You have plenty of applicants — and nobody you’d hire.

The apply button only reaches people who are actively looking, and that’s a small, oddly shaped slice of the market. The engineer you actually want is heads-down shipping somewhere else, not refreshing job boards. Volume isn’t your problem. Reach is.

3. Your favorite candidates keep taking other offers.

A strong engineer who starts interviewing is usually off the market in two to three weeks. If your process takes six, you’re not really hiring — you’re warming candidates up for whoever moves faster. Speed is a feature, and right now somebody else has it.

4. The hiring manager has become the recruiter.

If your engineering manager is sourcing on LinkedIn at nine at night, congratulations: you’ve hired the most expensive recruiter you’ll ever pay, and pulled them off the roadmap to do it. Managers are great at judging candidates. Asking them to find the candidates too is how both jobs get done at half quality.

5. You’re hiring for something you’ve never hired before.

First security hire, first ML engineer, first real engineering manager — whatever it is, you don’t know what great looks like yet, and learning it one interview at a time is slow and expensive. Calibration is exactly what an experienced third party brings on day one. It’s the difference between interviewing to learn and interviewing to decide.

6. The search needs to stay quiet.

Maybe you’re replacing someone who doesn’t know yet. Maybe the team doesn’t exist publicly. You can’t post a job you can’t talk about — but a headhunter can run the entire search without your company’s name appearing until exactly the moment you want it to.

7. Lots of interviews, no offers.

If the top of your funnel is busy but nothing ever closes, the candidates usually aren’t the problem. It’s the calibration, the process, or the offer — and it’s genuinely hard to tell which one from inside the building. An outside set of eyes can usually spot it within a week.

8. Nobody actually owns the search.

Recruiting done well is a full-time job. When it’s everyone’s side project, it’s no one’s priority — and that’s how a great candidate ends up sitting in a three-week silence, quietly concluding you’re not that interested. They’re not wrong, exactly. That’s the problem.

What to do next

Don’t sign anything. Really — the next step is a conversation, not a contract. Any headhunter worth working with will do a free intro call and tell you honestly whether they can help. Some of the most useful calls I’ve had ended with me saying “you don’t need me for this one.”

Come to that call with three things: the role, why it matters right now, and what’s already happened in the search so far. Fifteen minutes of that is worth more than any intake form ever invented.

Then ask a few pointed questions. How do they calibrate the technical bar — and whose bar is it? Where do their candidates actually come from? What happens if a placement doesn’t work out? If the answers are vague, keep looking. If they’re specific, you’ll feel the difference in about two minutes.

And if you’d like that conversation to be with a human — well, you already know where the button is.
