What a Human Headhunter Actually Does (That Your ATS Can’t)

Some honesty, from a guy whose whole brand is the word “human”: your applicant tracking system is good at its job. It remembers every candidate, moves them through stages, sends the emails on time, and never loses a résumé in somebody’s inbox. I use tools like it every day, happily. Automation done right is a beautiful thing — ask anyone who’s heard me talk about it for more than four minutes.

But here’s the distinction that gets lost: an ATS manages a hiring process. It doesn’t run a search. Those are two different jobs, and the second one is mostly made of things no dashboard will ever show you. So let me walk you through what actually happens behind a search — the parts you’re paying a headhunter for.

It starts with calibration, not keywords.

Before I contact a single candidate, I sit down with the hiring manager — and, whenever I can get them, the best engineers on the team. “Senior backend engineer” means five different things at five different companies, and the job description usually captures none of them. I’m not an engineer and I won’t pretend to be one; that’s exactly why I calibrate the technical bar with your people, so the bar is yours, not mine. Your ATS starts working after applications arrive. A real search starts working before the role is even finished being defined.

The best candidates never touch the apply button.

Most of the people I place weren’t looking when I reached out. They were heads-down, shipping, mildly annoyed at their roadmap, and completely invisible to any system that sits and waits for an application. Sourcing tools help me find them — I’m genuinely enthusiastic about the data side of this job — but a tool can only rank profiles. It can’t know that someone’s team is about to be reorganized, or that an engineer told me eight months ago she’d move for the right infrastructure role. And the message that actually gets a reply? It’s written by someone who read her work. Engineers can smell the difference in one sentence.

The screening call is where the search is won.

An algorithm can rank a résumé. It can’t hear the pause when I ask someone why they’re leaving, or catch the spark when they describe the project they can’t stop talking about. Those two signals — the hesitation and the spark — tell me more than the entire work-history section, and they’re the reason every candidate who reaches your calendar is worth your team’s interview hours. That part doesn’t automate. I’ve tried. (Kidding. Mostly.)

Someone has to say the honest thing.

Your ATS will never tell a hiring manager that the comp band is two years out of date, or that the seven-round interview loop is why the last three finalists vanished. And it will certainly never tell a candidate, kindly, that they bombed the system design round and exactly what to work on before the next one. Both conversations are uncomfortable. Both change outcomes. Candidates remember the recruiter who gave them a straight answer — and hiring managers make better decisions when someone in the room isn’t afraid to deliver the unflattering data point.

The offer dance is a human sport.

Here’s where deals actually die: the last ten days. The counteroffer that lands the night before signing. The comp expectation nobody surfaced in week one, because nobody asked. The partner with strong feelings about relocating. None of it shows up in a pipeline view, and nearly all of it is manageable — if someone saw it coming. A big part of my job is asking the awkward questions early so the ending is boring, then staying close through the resignation, the counter, and day one. A search isn’t over when the offer is signed. It’s over when they show up happy — and it’s going well when they’re still happy at the six-month check-in.

Where the tools stop.

So, plainly: keep the ATS. Keep the scheduling bot and the sourcing tools too — they’re excellent at memory and math, and anyone who tells you to throw them out is selling nostalgia. But hiring doesn’t run on memory and math. It runs on trust: a candidate trusting that what you told them about the job is true, a hiring manager trusting that the shortlist is real, both sides trusting someone enough to say the quiet thing out loud. Software hasn’t figured out trust yet. Until it does, you’ll want a human in the loop — ideally one who still picks up the phone.
